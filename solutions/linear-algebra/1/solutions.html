Solutions to some of the exercises in Sheldon Axler's Linear Algebra Done Right (4th edition).

Chapter 1

1. Show that $\alpha + \beta = \beta + \alpha$ for all $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}$.

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\beta = c + di$, where $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$. Then \[ \begin{align*} \alpha + \beta &= a + bi + c + di \\ &= (a + c) + (b + d)i \\ &= (c + a) + (d + b)i \\ &= c + di + a + bi \\ &= \beta + \alpha. \end{align*} \]   $\square$

2. Show that $(\alpha + \beta) + \lambda = \alpha + (\beta + \lambda)$ for all $\alpha, \beta, \lambda \in \mathbb{C}$

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\beta = c + di$, where $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\lambda = e + fi$, where $e, f \in \mathbb{R}$. Then \[ \begin{align*} (\alpha + \beta) + \lambda &= (a + c) + (b + d)i + e + fi \\ &= (a + e) + (b + f)i + c + di \\ &= \alpha + (\beta + \lambda). \end{align*} \]   $\square$

3. Show that $(\alpha \beta)\lambda = \alpha(\beta\lambda)$ for all $\alpha, \beta, \lambda \in \mathbb{C}$

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\beta = c + di$, where $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\lambda = e + fi$, where $e, f \in \mathbb{R}$. Then \[ \begin{align*} (\alpha \beta)\lambda &= [(a+bi)(c+di)](e+fi) \\ &= [(ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i](e + fi) \\ &= (ace - bde - adf - bcf) + (ade + bce + acf - dbf)i \\ &= a(ce - df) - b(de + cf) + a(de + cf)i + b(de - cf)i \\ &= a(ce - df + (de + ce)i) - b(de + cf + (de - cf))i \\ &= a[(c+di)(e+fi)] - b[(d+ci)(e+fi)] \\ \end{align*} \]   $\square$

4. Show that $\lambda(\alpha + \beta) = \lambda \alpha + \lambda \beta$ for all $\alpha, \beta, \lambda \in \mathbb{C}$

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\beta = c + di$, where $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\lambda = e + fi$, where $e, f \in \mathbb{R}$. Then \[ \begin{align*} \lambda(\alpha + \beta) &= \lambda(a + bi + c + di) \\ &= \lambda(a) + \lambda(b)i + \lambda(c) + \lambda(d)i \\ &= \lambda(a + bi) + \lambda(c + di) \\ &= \lambda \alpha + \lambda \beta. \end{align*} \]   $\square$

5. Show that for every $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$, there exists a unique $\beta \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $\alpha + \beta = 0$.

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$. Then there exists unique numbers $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $a+c=0$ and $b+d=0$. Namely, $c=-a$ and $d=-b$. Then, the unique number $\beta = -a - bi$ is such that \[ \begin{align*} \alpha + \beta &= (a + bi) + (-a - bi) \\ &= (a - a) + (b - b)i \\ &= 0 + 0i \\ &= 0. \end{align*} \]   $\square$

6. Show that for every $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ with $\alpha \neq 0$, there exists a unique $\beta \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $\alpha\beta = 1$.

Proof. Let $\alpha = a + bi$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ with $a \neq 0$ or $b \neq 0$. Let $\beta = c + di$, where $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$. Suppose $\alpha\beta = 1$. Then \[ \begin{align*} 1 = \alpha\beta &= (a + bi)(c + di) \\ &= (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i \end{align*} \] that is, $ac - bd = 1$ and $ad + bc = 0$. Therefore, $c = \frac{a}{a^2+b^2}$ and $d = -\frac{b}{a^2+b^2}$. That is, $\beta = \frac{a-bi}{a^2+b^2}$ is the unique number such that $\alpha\beta = 1$.   $\square$

7. Show that \[ \begin{align*} \frac{-1+\sqrt{3}i}{2} \end{align*} \] is a cube root of 1 (meaning that its cube equals 1).

Proof. \[ \begin{align*} \left(\frac{-1+\sqrt{3}i}{2}\right)^3 &= \frac{(-1+\sqrt{3}i)^3}{8} \\ &= \frac{9 -3\sqrt{3} + 3\sqrt{3} - 1}{8} \\ &= 1 \end{align*} \]   $\square$

8. Find two distinct square roots of $i$.

Proof. We are looking for $x, y \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $x^2 = i$ and $y^2 = i$. Recall that $i = e^{i\pi/2}$. Therefore, \[ \begin{align*} x &= (e^{i\pi/2})^{1/2} \\ &= e^{i\pi/4} \\ &= cos(\pi/4) + i sin(\pi/4)\\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align*} \] and \[ \begin{align*} y &= -(e^{i\pi/2})^{1/2} \\ &= e^{i\pi/4} \\ &= -cos(\pi/4) - i sin(\pi/4) \\ &= -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} - \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align*} \]   $\square$

9. Find $x\in \mathbb{R}$ such that \[ \begin{align*} (4, -3, 1, 7) + 2x = (5, 9, -6, 8) \end{align*} \]

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4)$, where $x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4 \in \mathbb{R}$. The resulting equation to solve is \[ \begin{align*} (4, -3, 1, 7) + 2(x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4) = (5, 9, -6, 8). \end{align*} \] That is, we have 4 linear equations: \[ \begin{align*} 4 + 2x_1 &= 5 \\ -3 + 2x_2 &= 9 \\ 1 + 2x_3 &= -6 \\ 7 + 2x_4 &= 8 \end{align*} \] Therefore, the unique solution is $x = (\frac{1}{2}, 6, -\frac{7}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$.   $\square$

10. Explain why there does not exist $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ such that \[ \begin{align*} \lambda(2 - 3i, 5 + 4i, -6 + 7i) = (12 - 5i, 7 + 22i, -32 - 9i). \end{align*} \]

Proof. Suppose there is, then from the first coordinate, we have \[ \begin{align*} \lambda(2 - 3i) = 12 - 5i &\iff \lambda = \frac{12 - 5i}{2 - 3i} \\ &\iff \lambda = \frac{(12 - 5i)(2 + 3i)}{(2 - 3i)(2 + 3i)} \\ &\iff \lambda = \frac{13(3 + 2i)}{13}\\ &\iff \lambda = 3 + 2i. \end{align*} \] Now, substituting this into the third coordinate, we have \[ \begin{align*} \lambda(-6 + 7i) &= (3 + 2i)(-6 + 7i) \\ &= -18 + 21i - 12i - 14 \\ &= -32 + 9i. \end{align*} \] Which means that the solution for the first and third coordinates are not the same, which is a contradiction.   $\square$

11. Show that $(x + y) + z = x + (y + z)$ for all $x, y, z \in \mathbb{F}^n$.

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$, $y = (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n)$, and $z = (z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_n)$. Then, \[ \begin{align*} (x + y) + z &= (x_1 + y_1, x_2 + y_2, \ldots, x_n + y_n) + (z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_n) \\ &= ((x_1 + y_1) + z_1, (x_2 + y_2) + z_2, \ldots, (x_n + y_n) + z_n) \\ &= (x_1 + (y_1 + z_1), x_2 + (y_2 + z_2), \ldots, x_n + (y_n + z_n)) \\ &= x + (y + z) \end{align*} \]   $\square$

12. Show that $(ab)x = a(bx)$ for all $x \in \mathbb{F}^n$ and all $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$.

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$. Then, \[ \begin{align*} (ab)x &= (ab)(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \\ &= ((ab)x_1, (ab)x_2, \ldots, (ab)x_n) \\ &= (a(bx_1), a(bx_2), \ldots, a(bx_n)) \\ &= a(bx) \end{align*} \]   $\square$

13. Show that $1x = x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{F}^n$.

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$, then \[ \begin{align*} 1x &= 1(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \\ &= (1x_1, 1x_2, \ldots, 1x_n) \\ &= (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \\ &= x. \end{align*} \]   $\square$

14. Show that $\lambda(x+y) = \lambda x + \lambda y$ for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{F}$ and all $x, y \in \mathbb{F}^n$.

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ and $y = (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n)$, then \[ \begin{align*} \lambda(x+y) &= \lambda(x_1 + y_1, x_2 + y_2, \ldots, x_n + y_n) \\ &= (\lambda(x_1+y_1), \lambda(x_2+y_2), \ldots, \lambda(x_n+y_n)) \\ &= (\lambda x_1 + \lambda y_1, \lambda x_2 + \lambda y_2, \ldots, \lambda x_n + \lambda y_n) \\ &= (\lambda x_1, \lambda x_2, \ldots, \lambda x_n) + (\lambda y_1, \lambda y_2, \ldots, \lambda y_n) \\ &= \lambda x + \lambda y. \end{align*} \]   $\square$

15. Show that $(a + b)x = ax + bx$ for all $x \in \mathbb{F}^n$ and all $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$.

Proof. Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$, then \[ \begin{align*} (a + b)x &= (a + b)(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \\ &= ((a + b)x_1, (a + b)x_2, \ldots, (a + b)x_n) \\ &= (ax_1 + bx_1, ax_2 + bx_2, \ldots, ax_n + bx_n) \\ &= (ax_1, ax_2, \ldots, ax_n) + (bx_1, bx_2, \ldots, bx_n) \\ &= ax + bx. \end{align*} \]   $\square$
